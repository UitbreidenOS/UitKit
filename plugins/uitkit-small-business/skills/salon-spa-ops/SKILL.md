# Salon and Spa Operations

## When to activate
- You run a salon, spa, barbershop, nail studio, or wellness practice and need to compress the operational work between appointments
- You're seeing no-show rates above 8% and want a structured recovery sequence
- Client retention is dropping — you want to identify which clients are at risk of not rebooking before they ghost
- You're launching a new service or seasonal package and need the announcement, the description, and the booking-page copy
- You want to systematize the work your front desk does so a new hire can do it on day one

## When NOT to use
- You have a dedicated marketing manager or front-desk lead who owns this work — they should keep ownership, not be replaced
- Your studio runs on word-of-mouth alone and you do no marketing — adding workflows here is solving a problem you don't have
- You're a sole practitioner with under 10 weekly appointments — at that volume, hand-managing each client relationship is the business

## Instructions

### Step 1: Set up your studio context

Give Claude the operational context once. Save it. You'll reuse it on every workflow.

Say:

"I run [salon / spa / barbershop / nail studio / wellness practice] in [city, neighborhood]. We have [N] service providers and offer [service types]. Our average ticket is [$X]. Our brand voice is [warm-and-welcoming / luxe / clinical-precise / fun-and-casual]. Our top three services by revenue are [list]. Our biggest competitor in the area is [name], and we differentiate on [specific reason]."

### Step 2: Build the no-show recovery sequence

No-shows are the single biggest revenue leak in salon and spa operations. Most studios let no-shows ghost without follow-up. Claude builds a structured 3-touch recovery:

Say:

"A client just no-showed an appointment. Draft a 3-touch recovery sequence: (1) same-day warm check-in, (2) 48-hour rebook offer with a small incentive, (3) 7-day "we miss you" with a fresh slot. Match my brand voice."

The first touch is warm and assumes the best ("hey, hope everything's okay — want to get you rebooked?"). The second is direct with an incentive. The third is the recovery attempt with a fresh hook. You review and adjust, then save these as templates your front desk uses every time someone no-shows.

### Step 3: Identify at-risk regulars

Once a quarter, pull a list of clients who used to come monthly but haven't booked in 60+ days. Paste their names and last-visit dates to Claude.

Say:

"These are 14 clients who used to come monthly. They haven't booked in 60+ days. Draft a personalized re-engagement message for each — reference how long they've been a client, the service they usually book, and an opening for them to rebook or share feedback if something went wrong."

Claude produces 14 personalized messages, each one referencing the specific client's history. You review, send the ones that feel right, and personally call the top 3 (the ones with the highest lifetime value).

### Step 4: Launch a new service or seasonal package

When you're launching, ask Claude:

"I'm launching a [new service / seasonal package] called [name]. It [describes what it is], costs [$X], and is for clients who [persona / use case]. Draft: (1) the booking-page description, (2) an announcement email to my client list, (3) three social posts for Instagram, (4) the in-studio menu insert copy."

You get a coordinated set of copy in one session. Review, polish, deploy.

### Step 5: Recall and rebooking automation

Most salons send generic "time for your next appointment" reminders. Claude personalizes:

Say:

"For each of these 20 clients whose last appointment was [service] on [date], draft a rebooking nudge that references the specific service they had, the recommended interval for that service, and offers two specific time slots."

The personalization lift is roughly 2-3x the response rate of generic reminders.

### Step 6: Service description refresh

Every 6 months, ask Claude to refresh your service menu descriptions. Old descriptions read like generic spa copy. Refreshed ones read like the studio's actual voice.

Say:

"Here are my current 12 service descriptions. Rewrite them in my brand voice, with each one being 30-60 words. Highlight what makes the experience specific to my studio, not a generic spa."

## Example

You run a 4-chair hair salon in Brooklyn. Average ticket is $185. You're seeing no-shows at about 9% — roughly 4 no-shows a week, which at your ticket size is $740 in lost weekly revenue, or $38K annually.

You set up the no-show recovery sequence with Claude. Three touches:

**Touch 1 (same day):** "Hey [name] — we missed you this morning at your 11am with Sarah. Hope everything's okay. We'd love to get you on the books for next week — want me to grab a slot for you?"

**Touch 2 (48 hours):** "Hi [name] — wanted to follow up on your missed appointment. We have a 20% courtesy on your next visit if you'd like to come in before [end of next week]. Sarah has Tuesday at 2pm and Thursday at 4pm — both yours if you want them."

**Touch 3 (7 days):** "Hi [name] — it's been a couple weeks. Whatever's going on, no judgment. If you want to come back in, here's a clean slot list for next week: [list]. If something didn't sit right last time, I'd genuinely like to know. — [Owner name]"

Your front desk runs this sequence on every no-show. In the first month, recovery rate moves from roughly 30% to roughly 65%. That's 2-3 extra appointments per week recovered, at $185 each. Annualized: $19-29K in recovered revenue.

The skill paid for the entire Claude subscription in week one.
