# Photography Studio

## When to activate
- You run a solo or small-team photography studio — wedding, portrait, commercial, real estate, food, brand
- Inquiry-to-booking conversion is dropping because your reply time is days, not hours
- You're sending contracts and shot lists from scratch every time instead of from a tuned template
- Gallery delivery emails are inconsistent and post-delivery reviews are spotty
- Off-season inquiry volume is dropping and you want a structured marketing push

## When NOT to use
- You shoot under 8 paid sessions per month and every inquiry gets a personal call — the workflow overhead doesn't pay back at that volume
- You work as a contracted photographer for a larger agency and don't own the client relationship
- You shoot for a corporate retainer (one client, ongoing work) — relationship-based workflows beat operational ones at this scale

## Instructions

### Step 1: Set up your studio context

Say:

"I run a [niche — wedding, portrait, brand, real estate, etc.] photography studio in [city]. My ideal client is [persona — couple type, family type, business type]. My signature style is [editorial / candid / classic / dramatic / clean-and-bright]. My pricing tiers are [list]. My typical booking lead time is [Y] months. My brand voice is [warm-and-personal / editorial-cool / craft-luxe / fun-and-energetic]."

### Step 2: Inquiry response automation

The biggest leak for most photographers is the 48-hour gap between inquiry and response. By the time you reply, the inquirer has emailed two other photographers.

Say:

"A new inquiry just came in for [event type] on [date] in [location]. The inquirer is [name], and they mentioned [specific detail from inquiry]. Draft: (1) a same-day response email that references their specific detail, links to my 2-3 most relevant past sessions, and proposes a 15-minute call this week with specific time slots, (2) a follow-up email for 48 hours later if no reply, (3) a 7-day final touch."

The personalization — referencing the specific detail they mentioned — converts at roughly 2x the rate of generic "thanks for reaching out, here's my pricing" replies.

### Step 3: Contract and shot list automation

Each session needs a contract, a shot list, and a pre-session prep email. Most photographers write these from scratch every time.

Say:

"This is a [session type] for [client] on [date] at [location]. The scope is [duration, deliverable counts]. Pricing is [$X]. Draft: (1) the contract in plain language (with my standard clauses around cancellation, image usage, and weather backup), (2) a session-specific shot list, (3) a pre-session prep email covering outfit guidance, timing, and what to expect."

You review, edit, send. Total time per session prep: 12 minutes instead of 45-60.

### Step 4: Gallery delivery emails

When the gallery is ready:

Say:

"I just finished editing [client]'s [session type] from [date]. The gallery has [N] images, delivered via [Pixieset / Pic-Time / ShootProof]. Draft: (1) the delivery email that references specific moments from the session, (2) a printing/album upsell that fits their package tier, (3) a soft review request to be sent 7 days later."

The personalization in delivery emails is what turns one-time clients into repeat clients and into referrals. Generic "your gallery is ready, here's the link" doesn't compound. Personalized, "I keep coming back to the photo of Mark laughing during the toast — second from the right in the reception set," does.

### Step 5: Off-season marketing push

Most photographers have a 3-4 month off-season. The studios that thrive use that period for active outreach instead of waiting for inbound.

Say:

"It's [off-season month]. My booking calendar for [busy season] is [X% booked]. Draft: (1) a referral campaign to past clients with an incentive, (2) a personal-outreach campaign to vendors I've worked with (planners, venues, MUAs), (3) social content for [N] weeks that previews the [busy season] availability, (4) a mini-session offer for the off-season itself if relevant to my niche."

### Step 6: Vendor and venue relationship building

For event photographers especially, relationships with planners and venues drive 40-60% of inbound. Most photographers don't actively cultivate these relationships.

Say:

"I worked with [planner / venue / coordinator] on [event date]. The work went [well / had issues — describe]. Draft: (1) a thank-you note that references a specific moment from the event and acknowledges their contribution, (2) a quarterly relationship-maintenance email I can send every 90 days, (3) a referral note that names what kind of clients we both serve well."

## Example

You're a wedding photographer in a mid-sized market. Average wedding fee: $4,200. You book about 28 weddings per year — $117K. Inquiries come in steadily but you reply within 48 hours typically. You suspect you're losing roughly 40% of inquiries to faster competitors.

You set up the inquiry response workflow. A new inquiry hits Monday at 6pm: "Hi! My fiancé and I are getting married October 14, 2027 at the Old Mill venue. We saw your work at our friend Sarah's wedding last May and loved your style — especially the candid family moments. We'd love to learn more about your pricing and availability."

By 7:15pm Monday, your reply is in their inbox. It references the Old Mill venue (you've shot there before — you mention this), references their friend Sarah's wedding (and you actually remember her), and proposes a 20-minute call Wednesday at 7pm or Thursday at 6:30pm.

They reply Tuesday morning and book the Thursday call. They book the wedding the following week.

Across 12 months, your inquiry-to-booking conversion moves from roughly 22% to 37%. At your inquiry volume (about 130/year), that's 19 extra bookings annually — but you can't shoot that many. So you raise prices instead. Average wedding fee moves from $4,200 to $5,600. Annual revenue: $156K vs $117K. The combination of faster reply + selective pricing flexibility produces $40K of incremental revenue — at a $240/year Claude cost.

You spend the additional capacity (because you book earlier) on the off-season vendor relationship workflow. The next year, vendor-referred inquiries grow from 18 to 41. Compounding.
