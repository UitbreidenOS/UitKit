# Objection Handling

## When to activate

You are in a live discovery call, email thread, or async response where a prospect has voiced resistance. Use this skill to convert objections into qualification opportunities rather than conversation endings. Applies specifically to B2B SaaS/tech sales cycles where you've already passed initial gatekeeping.

## When NOT to use

- During a first touchpoint or cold outreach (objections here usually mean "no context" — go back to pre-call qualification)
- For prospects who have explicitly unsubscribed or asked not to be contacted (honor the boundary)
- After a contract negotiation has stalled (escalate to deal team, not cold reframing)
- When objection is "we're switching vendors and have a signed contract" (prospect is unavailable for 12+ months)

---

## Instructions

### The ACI Framework: Acknowledge → Clarify → Indicate Next Step

All six objections follow this pattern:

1. **Acknowledge** (10 seconds) — Validate their statement without agreeing or disagreeing. This defuses defensive posture.
2. **Clarify** (30–60 seconds) — Ask a specific, open-ended question that reveals the real blocker beneath the surface objection.
3. **Indicate Next Step** (15 seconds) — Propose a concrete action tied to their answer, or leave a door open for future conversation.

### The Six Objections & Response Frameworks

---

#### Objection 1: "Just send me some information"

**What it really means:** "I'm not convinced this conversation is worth my time right now."

**Why people say it:**
- They want you gone without being rude
- They have genuine interest but are context-switching
- They think information alone will auto-educate them

**ACI Response Framework:**

| Phase | Response | Tone |
|-------|----------|------|
| **Acknowledge** | "Absolutely — I can send something over." | Friendly, no resistance |
| **Clarify** | "Before I do, what would make you actually *read* it? Is it [specific pain they mentioned in call] or [alternative pain from their industry]?" | Curious, not pushy |
| **Indicate** | "Perfect — I'll send a one-pager on that specific angle. When should I follow up — end of week?" | Concrete, time-bound |

**What to send (not what to send):**
- ✅ DO: A 1-page PDF with one specific problem + your angle on it + one case study of similar company
- ✅ DO: A 3-minute Loom video addressing their exact stated problem
- ❌ DON'T: Your deck (14+ slides, generic positioning)
- ❌ DON'T: Your company overview video (no frame of reference for them yet)
- ❌ DON'T: Generic "here are our product features" content

**When to persist (follow up) vs. concede:**
- Persist if: They named a specific problem in the call, asked a technical question, or work in your ICP
- Concede if: They said "maybe someday," gave monosyllabic answers throughout, or asked for info on a tangent product line

---

#### Objection 2: "We already have a solution for that"

**What it really means:** "I don't see why your thing is better, or I'm protecting my current choice."

**Why people say it:**
- They may genuinely have a tool (and it's working fine)
- They're testing you to see if you'll attack their vendor
- They've been burned by vendor pitch battles before

**ACI Response Framework:**

| Phase | Response | Tone |
|-------|----------|------|
| **Acknowledge** | "That's good — means you've already invested in solving it." | Supportive of their choice |
| **Clarify** | "Most of the teams we work with had [specific tool name] before too. The gap they kept hitting was [specific operational problem, not tool bashing]. Is that something you're running into — even if [tool] handles 80% of it?" | Sympathetic, specific |
| **Indicate** | "If not, you're probably in a good spot. But if it is, worth a quick conversation about how others worked around it. Fair?" | Low-pressure, gives them out |

**How to research their current tool (before/during the call):**
- Check their public tech stack (BuiltWith, G2, LinkedIn job posts)
- Ask in discovery: "What are you using today to solve [problem]?"
- Ask the real gap: "If you had to fix one thing about [tool], what would it be?"

**Key principle:** Never say "we're better than [tool]." Instead, say "we solve [specific use case] that [tool] wasn't built for."

**When to persist vs. concede:**
- Persist if: They describe a specific gap in their current setup, or they mention frustration with their vendor
- Concede if: They say "it works great for us," or your product and theirs don't actually overlap much

---

#### Objection 3: "This isn't a priority right now"

**What it really means:** "This is a nice-to-have, or there's something else more urgent."

**Why people say it:**
- They genuinely have a burning fire (migration, outage, funding round)
- Your problem is real but lower-ranked than 5 other initiatives
- They're waiting for approval from leadership before prioritizing

**ACI Response Framework:**

| Phase | Response | Tone |
|-------|----------|------|
| **Acknowledge** | "I get it — you're juggling a lot." | Empathetic |
| **Clarify** | "What would have to happen for it to move up the list? Is it a budget cycle, a Q roadmap review, or something specific firing — like [specific pain, e.g., 'losing engineering time to manual X']?" | Diagnostic, looking for trigger |
| **Indicate** | "Got it. So if I reconnect in [specific month], you'd have more clarity? Or is there a person on your team who *should* be thinking about this earlier?" | Pins a future date or suggests stakeholder escalation |

**The real blocker (uncover it):**
- Budget cycle? → "When does planning kick off for [next year]?"
- Competing project? → "When does [other project] wrap?"
- Waiting for headcount? → "When does hiring happen?"
- Regulatory/compliance? → "When does [audit/deadline] hit?"

**Timeline language to use:**
- "Worth reconnecting in [month], so you can bake it into the plan early" (vs. "let me check in later")
- "I'll send a 2-minute deck you can share with [stakeholder] — that way, when the convo happens, it's already on their radar"

**When to persist vs. concede:**
- Persist if: They give you a specific trigger date or stakeholder name
- Concede if: They say "2024 at the earliest" (too far out for a human sales follow-up cycle)

---

#### Objection 4: "No budget"

**What it really means:** "We didn't plan for this expense, or leadership hasn't approved discretionary spend."

**Why people say it:**
- Budget is actually locked (they're truthful)
- They don't see enough value to fight for the budget
- They're testing to see if you'll suddenly offer a discount

**ACI Response Framework:**

| Phase | Response | Tone |
|-------|----------|------|
| **Acknowledge** | "Makes sense — budgets are tight." | Never argue. Never discount without clarity. |
| **Clarify** | "When does your next planning cycle kick off? And is this something that would make the cut if we had the conversation earlier?" | Forward-looking, not defensive |
| **Indicate** | "Worth reconnecting [specific month] so it's in the plan from day one — before the budget's allocated." | Positions you as helping them plan, not hunting for scraps |

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Offer a discount ("we can do [lower number]")
- ❌ Suggest a "pilot" (code for "hoping they'll commit later")
- ❌ Say "ROI is so clear you'll find budget" (they've heard this before)

**What to do:**
- ✅ Get the next planning cycle date
- ✅ Ask if *another person* (CFO, founder, ops lead) should be in this conversation
- ✅ Send a "build it into the budget" template email in advance

**When to persist vs. concede:**
- Persist if: They give a specific planning cycle date (Q1 planning, January budget review, etc.)
- Concede if: They say "no budget" for the third time across different conversations (move to nurture, not active sequence)

---

#### Objection 5: "Not interested"

**What it really means:** "I don't see how this applies to me, or you haven't positioned it well yet."

**Why people say it:**
- They're busy and this doesn't feel relevant
- You led with features, not their problems
- They genuinely don't have the problem you solve

**ACI Response Framework:**

| Phase | Response | Tone |
|-------|----------|------|
| **Acknowledge** | "Fair — I get it." | No pushback, no sales tone |
| **Clarify** | "Can I ask one thing before we wrap? [Specific problem they'd face] — do you have a solution for that today, or is it just not a focus?" | One genuine question, leave them talking |
| **Indicate** | "Got it. If that does become a priority, I'm a quick message away. Fair?" | Permission-based, invites future re-engagement |

**The key phrase:** "Can I ask one thing before we wrap?" — This is permission to ask *without* it feeling like another pitch.

**Follow-up question variations:**
- "How are you handling [specific problem] today?"
- "What does [operational pain] actually cost you when it happens?"
- "If you could fix one thing about [their process], what would it be?"

**What gets people talking:**
- Specific, singular problem (not "how's your entire stack?")
- Framed as *their* problem, not your product fit
- Opens with "can I ask" rather than "let me tell you"

**When to persist vs. concede:**
- Persist if: They start answering your question — engagement signal
- Concede if: They say "not interested" a second time or give one-word answers to your clarifying question

---

#### Objection 6: "Bad timing"

**What it really means:** "You're calling at a chaos point, or I'm already committed elsewhere."

**Why people say it:**
- They literally just started a project / migration / fundraise
- They're ramping up / onboarding / in a crunch
- You called during their busiest quarter

**ACI Response Framework:**

| Phase | Response | Tone |
|-------|----------|------|
| **Acknowledge** | "Totally get it — I can tell you're heads down." | Respect their moment |
| **Clarify** | "If I reach out in [specific timeframe], would that work better? Or is there a better window — like after [specific project/deadline]?" | Propose a timeline, not vague |
| **Indicate** | "Perfect — I'll reconnect [specific date/month]. Good luck with [their project]." | Shows you were listening, leaves door open |

**Specific timeframes to offer (don't say "next quarter"):**
- "End of [month]?"
- "Once [their obvious project] wraps?"
- "Mid-[month] — does that feel better?"
- "I'll check in [date] — does that land?"

**Why this works:** You're not disappearing; you're respecting *their* timeline and showing up at a better moment.

**When to persist vs. concede:**
- Persist if: They give you a specific date or project name ("after we go live")
- Concede if: They say "way too early" (6+ months out, or no timeline at all)

---

### Decision Tree: Persist vs. Concede

```
Did they give you a specific trigger (date, event, stakeholder name)?
├─ YES → Persist (add to nurture, reconnect at date/event)
├─ NO → Did they engage with your clarifying question?
    ├─ YES (gave a real answer) → Persist (send relevant asset, schedule light follow-up)
    └─ NO (one-word answers, redirected) → Concede (move to lower-touch nurture)
```

**Concession language (respectful exit):**
- "Sounds like now's not the right moment — totally understand. I'll stay on your radar, and if it becomes relevant, just reach out. Fair?"
- "I get it. Want to stay connected on LinkedIn so you can loop me in when it makes sense?"

### Email vs. Call Response Differences

**On a Live Call:**
- ACI is real-time: acknowledge (5s) → clarify (ask and listen, 30s) → indicate (10s)
- You can read tone and adjust on the fly
- Silence after your clarifying question is OK — let them think
- If they go quiet, you ask again: "What would you say?"

**In Email:**
- Acknowledge in subject or first line
- Clarify with one specific question (not multiple)
- Indicate with a proposed action
- Keep to 4–6 sentences
- Include a specific call-to-action: "Reply with what would help" or "Let me know if [specific date] works"

**Example Email Response to "Send info":**
```
Subject: One specific thing to check on

Got it — I can definitely send something over.

Before I do, I want to make sure it's actually useful. When we talked, you mentioned [specific problem]. Is that the area you'd want me to focus on, or is it more about [alternative problem]?

Once I know, I'll send a one-pager specific to your situation instead of the generic deck.

Thoughts?

[Your name]
```

### Role-Play Format Example

**Setup:** SaaS vendor (you) calling a VP of Ops (prospect) who said "We already have a solution for that" in initial discovery.

---

**YOU:** "So you're using [tool name] today — how long have you been on it?"

**PROSPECT:** "About two years. Works pretty well for us."

**YOU:** "Got it. Most teams we work with had [tool name] as their starting point too. Usually where they hit friction was [specific operational gap — e.g., 'needing to pull data from five different places to get one single report']. Is that something you're dealing with, even if [tool name] handles most of it?"

**PROSPECT:** "Actually, yeah — we spend way too much time manually syncing data between [tool name] and our data warehouse."

**YOU:** "There it is. That's the exact problem we solve. How much time per week is that burning for you?"

**PROSPECT:** "Maybe 8–10 hours a week across the team."

**YOU:** "That's significant. If you could automate that, what would your team do with the time?"

**PROSPECT:** "Honestly, we could focus on strategy instead of data plumbing."

**YOU (Indicate Next Step):** "Makes sense. Here's what makes sense: I'll send you a two-minute video of how another ops team set this up — different industry, but same manual-sync problem. If it resonates, we can do a 20-minute technical call with our ops lead. Does that work?"

**PROSPECT:** "Sure, send it over."

---

**Key moves in this role-play:**
1. You didn't attack their current vendor ✅
2. You named a *specific operational gap*, not a vague feature ✅
3. You asked about impact (time, cost, distraction) ✅
4. You moved them toward a next step, not another pitch ✅

---

### Real Benchmarks

**If 30%+ of your calls hit the same objection**, that's a signal to shift your positioning earlier in the call (pre-emptively address it in your discovery questions).

**If a prospect says the objection twice in the same conversation**, assume concession — thank them, exit gracefully, add to nurture.

**Persistence ROI:** Prospects who give you a specific date/trigger and get a thoughtful follow-up have a 3–5x higher close rate than those you ask to "think about it" with no timeline.

## Example

### Real Scenario: Finance SaaS → Controller at Mid-Market E-commerce

**Company:** ZoomFinance (cash flow forecasting tool)  
**Prospect:** Controller, $50M e-commerce company  
**Call stage:** 20-minute discovery, first conversation

---

**PROSPECT (at 12 min mark):** "Look, this is interesting, but we already use Netsuite. Our accountant set it up, and it's handling our forecasting."

**YOU (Acknowledge → Clarify → Indicate):**

**ACKNOWLEDGE:** "Makes sense — Netsuite's a solid platform. How long have you been on it?"

**PROSPECT:** "About three years."

**YOU (Clarify):** "So here's what I see with a lot of finance teams on Netsuite — the forecasting module works great if you have a stable product line. But for e-commerce, where you're running multiple SKUs, promos, and seasonality... they usually end up doing the forecast in spreadsheets anyway because Netsuite doesn't handle variance. Is that what's happening for you?"

**PROSPECT:** "Actually, yes — we run three brands with different seasonal patterns. Netsuite can't model that, so we're pulling data and building forecasts in Excel. Takes forever."

**YOU (Indicate):** "There it is. That's exactly where we sit in — we take your Netsuite data and add the scenario modeling Netsuite doesn't do natively. Here's what I'd suggest: I'll send you a 3-minute walkthrough of how another e-commerce company set this up — they're similar scale, also three brands. If that resonates, we do a 20-minute call with our product lead to talk implementation. Fair?"

**PROSPECT:** "Yeah, send that over. What's your timeline for getting that to me?"

**YOU:** "Today — should be in your inbox in the next 30 minutes. And I'll send a Slack message Friday with a couple of time slots for next week. Cool?"

**PROSPECT:** "Works."

---

**What happened:**
- You didn't argue about Netsuite ✓
- You named *their specific problem* (seasonal SKU forecasting) ✓
- You positioned yourself as *complementary*, not competitive ✓
- You moved to a next step with specifics (video + call) ✓
- You've got a follow-up pattern (Friday slack with times) ✓

**Expected outcome:** 70%+ probability they watch the video. 40%+ probability they take the 20-minute call.

---

### Counter-Example: What Not to Do

**PROSPECT:** "We already have a solution for that."

**YOU (Bad response 1):** "Yeah, but we're way better than Netsuite. We have machine learning and—"

**PROSPECT (internally):** *Vendor is bashing my choice. I'm done.* **[Closes door mentally]**

---

**YOU (Bad response 2):** "No problem. Let me send you our deck so you can see all the features."

**PROSPECT (internally):** *More slides. No thanks.* **[Ignores email]**

---

**YOU (Bad response 3):** "Okay, well, when you're tired of paying so much for your current solution, reach out."

**PROSPECT (internally):** *Snarky. Not engaging.* **[Deletes contact info]**

---

**Why these fail:**
- They don't clarify the real gap → You're guessing, not solving
- They don't respect the prospect's current choice → You're building walls, not bridges
- They don't get permission to reconnect → You're hoping, not planning
