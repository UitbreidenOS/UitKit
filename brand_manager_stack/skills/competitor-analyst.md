# Competitor Analyst

## When to activate

At the start of campaign planning, when competitive landscape is changing, or when you need to find white space for new messaging. This skill researches 3–5 key competitors across positioning, messaging, recent moves, and customer sentiment. Output informs your messaging pillars and campaign angles.

## When NOT to use

Not for individual competitor mentions in content — use WebSearch for quick lookups. Not for competitive disparagement — this skill focuses on understanding their positioning, not attacking it. Not without a defined list of competitive set to analyze.

## Analysis Dimensions

**1. Market Positioning**
How does each competitor position themselves in customer minds? What's their core value prop? What market are they attacking? Who's their ICP?

**2. Messaging Pillars**
What are their 3–4 main messaging angles? What customer problems do they emphasize? What language do they use to differentiate?

**3. Recent Moves**
Product launches, new messaging, funding rounds, hire announcements, customer wins. What signals growth or pivot? What's the narrative they're building?

**4. Customer Sentiment**
G2, Capterra, Twitter, Reddit: What do customers say they do well? What complaints surface? Net sentiment: positive, neutral, negative?

**5. Go-to-Market Strategy**
How do they sell? Self-serve, sales-led, freemium, enterprise? Where do they spend marketing budget? What channels are they active on?

## Example

**Competitive Landscape Analysis**

**Date:** 2026-06-12
**Analysis Scope:** Competitor A, Competitor B, Competitor C
**Your Company:** We focus on developer experience and integration velocity

---

## Positioning Matrix

| | Developer-First | Enterprise-First |
|---|---|---|
| **Price-Conscious** | [Us] | [Competitor A] |
| **Feature-Rich** | [Competitor B] | [Competitor C] |

---

## Competitor Profiles

### Competitor A: EnterpriseSoft

**Positioning:** "The enterprise platform for large teams"
**ICP:** Fortune 500, 1000+ employees
**Value Prop:** "Complete solution with white-glove support"

**Messaging Pillars:**
1. Compliance & Security — SOC 2, HIPAA, FedRAMP
2. Enterprise Support — dedicated account manager
3. Custom Integration — "we'll build it for you"

**Recent Moves:**
- Series D ($50M) announced March 2026 — "expanding into APAC"
- Hired 20 sales reps — "scaling enterprise sales"
- Case study: Goldman Sachs implementation

**Customer Sentiment:** 4.1/5 on G2, but complaints about "slow onboarding," "inflexible pricing," "poor API docs"

**GTM Strategy:** Sales-led, conferences, industry analysts

**Strengths:** Enterprise credibility, compliance focus
**Weaknesses:** Slow product updates, poor developer experience, expensive
**Opportunities for Us:** Developers hate their setup process; we can own "fast integration"

---

### Competitor B: DevToolCo

**Positioning:** "The developer-first platform"
**ICP:** Startups and growth-stage, 10–100 engineers
**Value Prop:** "Ship faster with less friction"

**Messaging Pillars:**
1. Speed — "integrate in hours, not weeks"
2. Flexibility — "works with your stack"
3. Community — "thousands of developers, free tier"

**Recent Moves:**
- Product launch: new API (June 2026) — "10x faster"
- GitHub sponsorship announcement
- 50K+ developer community milestone

**Customer Sentiment:** 4.7/5 on G2, praised for "amazing docs," "responsive team," complaints about "pricing jumps at scale," "enterprise features lacking"

**GTM Strategy:** Community-first, developer marketing, free tier funnel

**Strengths:** Developer love, strong community, transparent pricing
**Weaknesses:** No enterprise features, small sales team, limited security posture
**Opportunities for Us:** Enterprise developers want DevToolCo's UX with EnterpriseSoft's security; we own the middle

---

## White-Space Analysis

**Positioning Gaps:**
- No competitor addresses "scaling from startup to enterprise without rip-and-replace"
- No competitor emphasizes "transparent, developer-forward pricing"

**Customer Segment Gaps:**
- Mid-market tech companies (50–500 engineers) feel caught between startup tools and enterprise bloat

**Messaging Gaps:**
- "We grow with you" narrative (not: "start lean" or "scale large," but both)
- "Security that doesn't slow you down"

**Channel Gaps:**
- Competitors focus on conferences or GitHub; we can own "developer education on YouTube and Substack"

---

## Recommended Messaging Angles

1. **"Enterprise-grade security at startup speed"** — Own the middle market that Competitor A underserves with overpriced overhead and Competitor B underserves with lacking compliance features.

2. **"Grow without rip-and-replace"** — Emphasize that companies migrating from competitors don't need to rewrite their integration; position as the bridge from startup tools to enterprise.

3. **"Transparent pricing. No surprise enterprise tax."** — Lean into Competitor B's strength (developer love) and Competitor A's weakness (opacity). Own clarity.
